Section 1: Introduction

Lacrosse Sixes is the shortened-format variant of lacrosse developed by World Lacrosse to provide a faster, smaller-field, more spectator-friendly version of the game suitable for international and Olympic competition. The format was approved for the 2028 Los Angeles Olympic Games as the lacrosse medal discipline — the sport's return to the Olympic program after a 1908 hiatus. Sixes uses 6-on-6 (5 field players + 1 goalie), shorter games (4 × 8-minute quarters), a smaller field, and a 30-second shot clock. The current ruleset is the World Lacrosse Sixes Rules v1.1 (December 2024). See the sibling World Lacrosse Official Rulebook, NCAA Men's Lacrosse Rules, and NCAA Women's Lacrosse Rules entries for the parent ruleset and college variants.

Section 2: Equipment

  • Stick (crosse): 40-46" (102-117 cm) for field players (unified men's / women's stick), max 72" for goalie
  • Ball: standard lacrosse ball (140-149 g, ~6.3 cm diameter), white preferred
  • Helmet: required for all players (men's-style helmet with full face mask)
  • Mouthguard: mandatory
  • Gloves: required for all field players + goalie
  • Protective pads: shoulder, elbow, rib pads required; less protective than men's traditional field lacrosse (game emphasizes speed over collision)
  • Goalkeeper: additional chest protector + throat guard + helmet

Section 3: Playing Area

  • Field: 70 m × 36 m (~76 × 39 yards) — substantially smaller than traditional men's lacrosse (110 × 60 m) or women's (100 × 55 m)
  • Goal: 1.83 m × 1.83 m (6 ft × 6 ft) — same as field lacrosse
  • Crease: 2.75 m radius circle around each goal
  • Restraining boxes near each goal
  • No traditional offside line — Sixes is fluid, no offside rule

Section 4: Players & Officials

  • 6 per side: 5 field players + 1 goalkeeper
  • Roster: 12 players per game (6 on field, 6 substitutes); unlimited substitutions on-the-fly
  • Officials: 3-person crew (referee + 2 umpires) at international/Olympic level
  • Game director + technical officials at championship events

Section 5: Rules of Play

Game Length

  • 4 quarters × 8 minutes running time (35 minutes total including breaks)
  • 2-minute breaks between quarters; 5-minute halftime
  • OT: golden goal sudden-death (4-minute periods)

Starting Play

  • Each quarter begins with a "draw" at center (similar to women's lacrosse) — players from each team contest possession
  • After a goal, the conceding team's goalkeeper restarts play

Possession + Shot Clock

  • 30-second shot clock — offense must shoot within 30 seconds of gaining possession
  • Failure to shoot in time → turnover
  • No traditional "stalling" or "keep-away" — shot clock enforces pace

Movement

  • No offside rule — players move freely across field
  • Goalkeeper may participate in play across the field (within crease for goalie privileges)
  • Body contact substantially reduced vs men's field lacrosse — Sixes emphasizes stick checks, not body checks

Section 6: Scoring

  • 1 goal = 1 point (ball fully crosses goal line, in-bounds, scored from outside crease)
  • Total game goals decides winner
  • OT: golden-goal sudden-death
  • Tournament: knockout bracket with round-robin pool play

Section 7: Violations & Penalties

  • Personal foul (slashing, illegal body check, cross-check, etc.): 30-second timed penalty (player serves in penalty box; team plays short)
  • Technical foul (interference, illegal screen, hold, etc.): change-of-possession or 30-second penalty
  • Crease violation: turnover
  • Shot-clock violation: turnover
  • Misconduct (yellow / red card): yellow = 2-minute penalty; red = ejection + 5-minute penalty served by teammate
  • Excessive contact: stricter enforcement than field lacrosse — Sixes is designed to minimize collisions

Section 8: Safety Considerations

Lacrosse Sixes is designed with reduced-contact rules vs men's field lacrosse — stick checks remain but body checks are heavily restricted. Mandatory helmet + mouthguard + full pad suite. World Lacrosse requires on-site medical (athletic trainer minimum, team physician at international tournament). Concussion protocol: WL Concussion Management Guidelines mirror World Rugby framework — removal-for-evaluation, multi-stage return-to-play, mandatory rest. Heat protocols for summer-season events (mandatory water breaks every quarter, heat-stop authority). AED on-site at all sanctioned tournaments. Goalkeeper-specific protective requirements (chest protector, throat guard, full face mask) enforced strictly.

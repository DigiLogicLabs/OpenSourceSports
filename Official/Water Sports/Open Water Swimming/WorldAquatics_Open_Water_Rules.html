Section 1: Introduction

Open Water Swimming is the discipline of competitive long-distance swimming in natural bodies of water — oceans, lakes, rivers, reservoirs — governed internationally by World Aquatics (renamed from FINA in January 2023). The Olympic marathon swim (10 km) was added to the program at Beijing 2008. World Aquatics-sanctioned distances are 5 km, 10 km, and 25 km, plus team relays (4 × 1.25 km mixed-gender) at the World Championships. Open water differs from pool swimming in several core ways: drafting and contact are allowed, navigation is the swimmer's responsibility, and weather + water conditions are part of the competition.

Section 2: Equipment

  • Swimsuit: World Aquatics-approved suit (textile-only since 2010); men's suit max from waist to knee, women's max from shoulder to knee
  • Cap: mandatory event-issued numbered cap (high-visibility colors)
  • Goggles: permitted (anti-glare recommended for sun)
  • Wetsuit: permitted at water temperatures below 18°C (mandatory below 16°C); prohibited above 20°C; optional 18-20°C
  • Body markings: race number marked on both upper arms in waterproof ink
  • No fins, paddles, or buoyancy aids; no electronic timing/positioning aids carried by swimmer

Section 3: Playing Area

  • Course: closed loop typically 1.25 km or 2.5 km, multi-lap for 10 km and 25 km
  • Turning buoys: large inflatable navigation buoys at each corner; swimmers must round on prescribed side
  • Feeding station: floating dock or pontoon where coaches can pass food/drink to swimmers via long poles (10 km and 25 km only)
  • Water temperature: minimum 16°C (race may be cancelled below); maximum 31°C (race may be cancelled above)
  • Course depth: minimum 1.4 m for safety

Section 4: Players & Officials

  • Individual race (mass start) at 5 km / 10 km / 25 km
  • Team relay: 4 swimmers (2M + 2W mixed) × 1.25 km each at World Championships
  • Officials: referee, course umpires (on motorized boats), feeding-station judge, timing officials
  • "Yellow card / red card" warning system enforced by course umpires on the water
  • Independent finish-line camera (touch pad at finish gantry)

Section 5: Rules of Play

Start

  • Mass start: all swimmers enter water at signal (typically from pontoon dive-in)
  • False start rules apply — second offense = disqualification

During the Race

  • Drafting allowed — swimmers may swim directly behind another to reduce drag
  • Contact tolerated provided not deliberately obstructive (no pulling, no submerging opponents)
  • Swimmer responsible for own navigation — must round each turning buoy on prescribed side
  • Feeding station: coaches may pass food/drink via long pole; swimmer must collect without leaving water or holding pole

Finish

  • Race finishes at a touch pad on a floating gantry — swimmer's hand contact registers final time
  • Photo-finish protocol for close finishes

Section 6: Scoring

  • Individual race: lowest elapsed time wins
  • Top-10 typically separated by <30 seconds in elite 10 km races
  • World Cup series: round points → series standings
  • Olympic qualification: based on prior World Championship + continental qualifier results
  • Marathon Swim World Series ranking maintained by World Aquatics

Section 7: Violations & Penalties

  • Yellow card: warning (impeding, holding, kicking deliberately, illegal feed)
  • Red card: disqualification
  • Two yellows: automatic disqualification
  • False start (2nd offense): disqualification
  • Cutting course (wrong side of buoy): red card
  • Receiving outside assistance: red card (other than authorized feeding-station hand-off)
  • Doping: WADA Code; World Aquatics-administered testing program

Section 8: Safety Considerations

Open water swimming presents unique risks: hypothermia, hyperthermia, drowning, jellyfish/marine-life contact, navigation hazards. World Aquatics mandates: water-temperature pre-race measurement (race cancellation thresholds at 16°C and 31°C); on-water rescue boats every ~200 m of course; spotters on shore + on boats; safety swimmers in water; on-site medical (ambulance + EMT + team physician); helicopter evacuation plan for remote venues. Mandatory pre-race medical clearance + cardiac screening. Buddy / partner-pairing system between coaches and athletes at each feeding station. AED at finish line. Concussion / aspiration protocol after any submersion incident — swimmer pulled, medical evaluation, conditional return-to-water authorization required. World Aquatics suspended/postponed events for water quality (e.g., Paris 2024 Seine deliberations) — water quality testing pre-race may delay or cancel competition.
